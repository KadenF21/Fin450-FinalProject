Methodology

The data that is used for this analysis has been scraped from official Government of Alberta sources, location data has been scraped from Nominatim Opens street map. Government of Alberta sources include; 2006-2025 historical wildfire data, and Alberta Regional data. The Alberta Regional data includes the top and bottom ten values for both median family income and percentage of first nations in the municipality. Median family income that was reported at $0 was excluded as it is erroneous.

Limitations

This analysis has some limitations; firstly some data may be wrongly reported. For example, the minimum wildfire size that can be reported is .1 hectares, however any fire that is responded to by wildfire assets- is considered a wildfire. This creates reporting issues as car fires may be inaccurately listed as wildfires. As such, for a majority of this report only fires with areas larger than .11 hectares will be considered. Furthermore as per definition only fires that are started by; Lightning, restart or undetermined- will be considered as natural fires with the rest being considered human caused.

Fire Size Proximity to City

Time and distance are the most important factors when creating wildfire policy. Time is important as fires grow at an exponential rate, the sooner a response is initiated the better. Distance is just as important as well as it determine the rate of which resources can respond to the fire. This plot below showcases the relationship between each fire and the respective distance to the city, large fires are defined by any fire with an area larger than 2000 hectares while small is anything less than 2000.

As you can see above, no real real visual relationship occurs. If anything there are more large fires closer to cities than further, which indicates that fires are not being left alone to grow. To further test this, the static correlation between the fire sizes and proximity to the city was calculated, with a value of .025 which supports the initial idea.

Machine Learning Model 2 - Extreme Gradient Boost

Continuing on, the second model being utilized is an Extreme Gradient Boost regression model. As this model contains many non linear relationships; such as wind direction and fuel type- a more robust model is needed. The same predictors are being utilized for this model.

As shown above, relative humidity is the most important feature. This is to be expected as dry material is more easily flammable and spreads faster. Both location and wind speed are important factors as well, location will get explored later in the map section and wind speed affects wildfires in multiple ways; adding oxygen to fires and it also creates wind desiccation which causes moisture to be sucked out of plants- creating a very negative fire environment (RHS, 2026)! C2 Fuel type is characterized as boreal spruce, or evergreen forests, as evergreen forests are prone to desiccation and produce a very flammable resin. The final point of interest is month 5, may- this is widely reported to be one of the most prominent fire activity months as temperature begins to rise and snow melts. Knowing these factors gives additional insight into how to spot the warning signs for a potential fire.

This model is a modest improvement from the original linear regression, with an R^2 value 5% higher, and lower error.

Machine Learning Conclusion

While there are large gaps in the model such as nearly 80% of variance being unexplained, these three models combined may prove useful as a basis for flagging dangerous conditions. To summarize, there are three red flag factors to pay attention to; Coniferous fuels, high winds, and low moisture. Areas that have these conditions should be on high alert, and ready to respond to any wildfires.

Interactive Map

When the data of the previous graphs gets inputted onto a map, the story becomes much more clear.

As shown above, there is a large variation in where high/low income municipalities are situated, and non first nations municipalities are situated in areas where no wildfires occur due to geography, in fact they are located within an area called “Non Forest Protection Area”- as such there are no wildfires.

Areas with a high First Nations population are reserves, typically found in northern/rural Alberta. Unfortunately, due to the isolation aspect many of these areas have slow fire response times. This does present an opportunity for both the province and the First Nations.

Recommendation 1

Employment opportunities are scarce on reserves according to indigenous Services and Statistics Canada, with an employment rate of 47% compared to the national average of 74%. One way to combat this is through employment opportunities within Wildfire firefighting and support. Currently the province employs firefighters through the “Firetack” program however historically this program has been more like exploitation than employment. The province contracts eight person crews that function similarly to wage wild land firefighters, no official pay grid is available to the public.

In the most recent Alberta Wildfire Payment Directive pay is listed for emergency non-contract hiring. Some notable figures are the Firetack Leader and member which are listed at $22.08 and $20.97 per hour respectfully. As of April 1st 2026, the lowest a wage wildland firefighter member can make is $23.80 an hour.

As the province is facing extreme turnover for seasonal positions, it should look towards a more sustainable employment program with First Nations- a compromise that would benefit both parties.

Recommendation 2

There should be a greater emphasis on preventive measures to ensure rural fires don’t remain undetected. With advancements in drone and infrared technology, tower observers could be trained and equipped with drones that could help survey the land and detect any hot-spots or early start fires. Large scale wildfires can cost millions of dollars a day, whereas state of the art drones may cost a fraction of that, between $10-20,000.

Conclusion

Wildfires will continue to be a part of every Albertan’s life as they are a natural occurrence and can’t completely be prevented. What can be changed is how prepared and proactive the province is, doing so can; prevent damages, create employment opportunities and invest in future technology.
